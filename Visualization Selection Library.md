# **Visualization Selection Library**

## **Purpose**

Choose the simplest visualization that communicates the insight accurately.

When multiple chart types can communicate the same insight, choose the one that requires the least cognitive effort.

---

# **Tabular Analysis**

## **Tables**

Use when:

* Exact values matter  
* Users need detailed inspection

Best for:

* Financial reports  
* Operational reporting

---

## **Pivot Tables**

Use when:

* Users need dynamic exploration  
* Multiple dimensions require grouping

Best for:

* Business intelligence  
* Ad hoc analysis

---

# **Trend Analysis**

## **Line Charts**

Use when:

* Showing change over time  
* Comparing trends

Examples:

* Revenue growth  
* Traffic trends

---

## **Area Charts**

Use when:

* Showing trends plus cumulative volume

Examples:

* Market share evolution  
* Revenue composition

---

## **Sparklines**

Use when:

* Showing compact trends within dashboards

Examples:

* KPI monitoring  
* Executive scorecards

---

# **Category Comparison**

## **Bar Charts**

Use when:

* Comparing categories

Examples:

* Revenue by region  
* Sales by product

---

## **Horizontal Bar Charts**

Use when:

* Labels are long  
* Ranking matters

---

## **Grouped Bar Charts**

Use when:

* Comparing multiple groups side-by-side

Example:

* Revenue by product across years

---

## **Stacked Bar Charts**

Use when:

* Showing totals and composition simultaneously

---

## **Percent Stacked Bars**

Use when:

* Showing relative proportions

---

## **Waterfall Charts**

Use when:

* Explaining how a value changed

Examples:

* Profit bridges  
* Budget variance

---

## **Pareto Charts**

Use when:

* Identifying the few causes responsible for most outcomes

Based on the 80/20 principle.

---

# **Part-to-Whole Relationships**

## **Donut Charts**

Use when:

* Categories are limited  
* High-level composition is needed

Maximum:

* 3–6 segments

---

## **Treemaps**

Use when:

* Comparing proportions across many categories

Advantages:

* More scalable than pie charts

---

## **Waffle Charts**

Use when:

* Showing percentages in a highly intuitive format

---

# **Relationship Analysis**

## **Scatter Plots**

Use when:

* Analyzing correlation between two variables

Examples:

* Ad spend vs revenue  
* Age vs spending

---

## **Bubble Charts**

Use when:

* Comparing three variables

Examples:

* Revenue, profit, and market size

---

## **Heat Maps**

Use when:

* Showing density or intensity

Examples:

* Survey results  
* User activity

---

# **Distribution Analysis**

## **Histograms**

Use when:

* Understanding frequency distributions

Examples:

* Call durations  
* Order values

---

## **Box Plots**

Use when:

* Comparing distributions  
* Finding outliers

---

## **Violin Plots**

Use when:

* Showing density and distribution together

Mostly used in:

* Statistics  
* Research

---

## **KDE Plots**

Use when:

* Displaying smooth probability distributions

---

# **Hierarchical Data**

## **Tree Diagrams**

Use when:

* Showing parent-child relationships

Examples:

* Org charts  
* File systems

---

## **Sunburst Charts**

Use when:

* Showing hierarchical proportions

Examples:

* Budget allocations  
* Navigation structures

---

# **Performance Monitoring**

## **Bullet Charts**

Use when:

* Comparing actual vs target performance

Advantages:

* More informative than gauges

Best for:

* KPI dashboards

---

# **Geographic Analysis**

## **Choropleth Maps**

Use when:

* Comparing values across regions

Examples:

* Revenue by country  
* Population density

---

## **Cartograms**

Use when:

* Geographic size should represent value

Use carefully because geography becomes distorted.

---

# **Project Management**

## **Gantt Charts**

Use when:

* Visualizing schedules  
* Tracking milestones  
* Managing dependencies

---

# **Flow Analysis**

## **Flow Charts**

Use when:

* Documenting workflows

Examples:

* Customer journeys  
* Operational processes

---

## **Sankey Diagrams**

Use when:

* Showing movement between stages

Examples:

* Funnels  
* Budget allocations  
* Energy flows

---

# **Network Analysis**

## **Force-Directed Graphs**

Use when:

* Visualizing relationships between entities

Examples:

* Social networks  
* Infrastructure maps

---

## **Chord Diagrams**

Use when:

* Showing interactions between categories

Examples:

* Trade flows  
* Resource transfers

---

# **Specialized Visualizations**

## **Radar / Spider Charts**

Use when:

* Comparing multiple dimensions for a small number of entities

Examples:

* Skill assessments  
* Product comparisons

Use cautiously because interpretation can become difficult.

---

## **Candlestick Charts**

Use when:

* Showing financial market activity

Displays:

* Open  
* High  
* Low  
* Close

---

# **Visualization Selection Rule**

Start with:

1. Table  
2. Bar Chart  
3. Line Chart

Only move to more complex visualizations if they provide additional insight that simpler visualizations cannot communicate effectively.

Complexity should be earned, not assumed.
